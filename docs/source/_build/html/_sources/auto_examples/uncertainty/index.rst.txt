Uncertainty
===========

This gallery focuses on **forecast uncertainty and calibration** in
GeoPrior.

The examples in this section explain how uncertainty-aware forecast
outputs should be inspected, compared, and interpreted. They are
designed to help readers move from interval forecasts to practical
questions such as:

- are the prediction intervals calibrated,
- how does coverage trade against sharpness,
- how well do quantile-based forecasts score exceedance events?

In other words, these pages teach how to understand the reliability
of GeoPrior forecasts rather than just their central predictions.

What this section teaches
-------------------------

The examples in this folder are designed as **uncertainty lessons**.

Each page usually does four things:

1. create or load a compact synthetic uncertainty-aware input,
2. call the relevant GeoPrior uncertainty plotting helper,
3. inspect the produced visual or tabular result,
4. explain how to interpret calibration and forecast reliability.

The main goal is to make interval behavior and forecast reliability
intuitive and reproducible.

How to read this gallery
------------------------

A good way to use this section is to move from:

- interval calibration,
- to coverage-versus-sharpness trade-offs,
- to exceedance-oriented event scoring.

That order mirrors the usual uncertainty-reading workflow:

1. are the intervals calibrated,
2. are they too wide or too narrow,
3. do they produce useful event probabilities?

Examples in this section
------------------------

``plot_interval_calibration.py``
    Examine whether forecast intervals are calibrated and how the
    predicted quantiles align with observed behavior.

``plot_coverage_vs_sharpness.py``
    Compare reliability against interval width to understand the
    practical trade-off between safe and informative forecasts.

``plot_brier_exceedance.py``
    Evaluate exceedance-style event probabilities using Brier scores
    derived from quantile forecasts.

Practical workflow
------------------

A common workflow through this section is:

1. generate or load uncertainty-aware forecast outputs,
2. inspect interval calibration,
3. compare coverage and sharpness,
4. assess exceedance-oriented forecast usefulness,
5. then move on to summary builders or final figure generation.

This separation is deliberate. It helps keep:

- forecasting,
- uncertainty assessment,
- and downstream reporting

cleanly distinct.

Notes
-----

- These examples are intentionally compact and lesson-oriented.
- The pages in this section are uncertainty-first: they explain how
  to judge reliability, not just central accuracy.
- A good rule of thumb is:

  - ``forecasting/`` explains what is predicted,
  - ``uncertainty/`` explains how reliable that prediction is,
  - ``tables_and_summaries/`` builds reusable uncertainty products,
  - ``figure_generation/`` turns them into final visual results.
